DOPAMINE ARCHITECTURE MASTERCLASS

Jonathan delivered an unsolicited 1,500-word treatise on the neuroscience of engagement — the single most actionable piece of external feedback the Academy has received. His core thesis: "Dopamine isn't released when you 'get' a reward — it's released in 'anticipation' of one."

1. Variable Reward Loops

Don't give predictable rewards — vary them. A student who answers correctly might get a standard point, or might unlock a rare "Civilisational Relic," or trigger a cinematic moment. The uncertainty keeps engagement dramatically higher than fixed rewards.

2. Progress Bar Obsession

The Zeigarnik Effect: a progress bar at 85% is almost physically uncomfortable to leave. "11 of 12 nodes complete" will pull people back. iAAi's 12-relay structure is perfectly suited to this.

3. Streaks & Loss Aversion

Loss aversion is roughly twice as powerful as reward anticipation neurologically. Duolingo exploited this brilliantly. A "Civilisational Streak" mechanic for iAAi would be potent.

4. The Curiosity Gap

George Loewenstein's theory: the brain experiences a gap between what it knows and what it wants to know as mild but persistent discomfort. "Why did the Mongols control the Silk Road but build none of it?" — perfect curiosity gap opener.

5. Mastery & Identity

When a learner starts to think "I am someone who understands infrastructure" — that identity becomes self-reinforcing. Titles, ranks, and roles (Pioneer, Scholar, Master) tap this directly. iAAi's existing tier system is well conceived.

"The best educational game designers talk about 'chocolate-covered broccoli' — and warn against it. The mistake is making learning the broccoli and fun the chocolate coating. The goal is to make the learning itself the dopamine hit. When understanding 'why the Roman road network was the actual source of Roman military dominance' feels like a revelation rather than a lesson — that's when iAAi wins."
— Jonathan Green, 9 March 2026
